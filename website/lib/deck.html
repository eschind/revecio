ReveWhy it's broken

The status quo

Analyst-heavy, premium-priced, structurally underperforming.

  • High-cost, human-intensive. Every IC package, monitoring report, scenario analysis, and board deck is produced manually. Technology leverage is near zero.
  • Premium pricing. Sub-$250M clients pay 30–40 bps blended; fully loaded fees reach 75–175 bps — consuming budget that would otherwise fund mission.
  • Dispersion and underperformance. 8% spread between top- and bottom-quartile OCIO returns over three years through 2025 — a 25% compounded gap. Bottom quartile returned just 3% in 2024.
  • Opacity and conflicts. Hidden fees, cherry-picked track records, and compensation routed through affiliated products are pervasive.
  • Smaller institutions excluded. ~60% of the $1.25T sub-$250M segment is unaddressed by any third-party investment management service.

ReveThe underserved segment

Where we start

A huge opportunity among
underserved sub-$250M institutions.

Only ~40% use an investment advisor
Of the U.S. sub-$250M institutional segment, only about 40% engages an OCIO or investment consultant at all. The rest is self-managed, run by volunteers, or served by bank trust departments and RIAs.
$1.25T across more than 20k institutions
Community foundations, school and college endowments, arts/cultural organizations, and hospital foundations between $10M and $250M. A vast, addressable pool with no real investment partner.
Legacy OCIO unit economics
Under current unit economics, incumbent firms can't profitably serve smaller clients — so they push them into one-size pooled vehicles, low-touch consulting, or don't serve them at all.

At Reve's 15–20 bps pricing, this segment alone represents a $2B+ annual revenue opportunity where we can offer bespoke, high-end OCIO services at fees no incumbent can match.

What they're up against

ReveWhy now

OCIO is the next frontier for agentic AI

OCIO is information labor,
not judgment labor.

Gathering market and manager data, building and stress-testing portfolios, drafting IPS documents, monitoring positions, generating board materials, answering trustee questions — all dense information processing, all today done by hand.

Frontier agentic systems with the right data foundation and embedded domain knowledge can do nearly all of it — with full audit trails. Final judgments stay with humans.

Agentic OCIO is harder than agentic legal or accounting — more degrees of freedom, fewer clean right answers. That difficulty is the moat.
